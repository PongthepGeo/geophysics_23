1.1) The Law of Universal Gravitation

Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1727) was born in the same year in which Galileo died. Unlike Galileo, who relished debate, Newton was a retiring person and avoided con- frontation. His modesty is apparent in a letter written in 1675 to his colleague Robert Hooke, famous for his exper- iments on elasticity. In this letter Newton made the famous disclaimer “if I have seen further (than you and Descartes) it is by standing upon the shoulders of Giants.” In modern terms Newton would be regarded as a theoretical physicist. He had an outstanding ability to synthesize experimental results and incorporate them into his own theories. Faced with the need for a more powerful technique of mathematical analysis than existed at the time, he invented diﬀerential and integral calculus, for which he is credited equally with Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibnitz (1646–1716) who discovered the same method independently. Newton was able to resolve many issues by formulating logical thought experiments; an example is his prediction that the shape of the Earth is an oblate ellipsoid. He was one of the most outstanding syn- thesizers of observations in scientific history, which is implicit in his letter to Hooke. His three-volume book Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica, published in 1687, ranks as the greatest of all scientific texts. The first volume of the Principia contains Newton’s famous Laws of Motion, the third volume handles the Law of Universal Gravitation. The first two laws of motion are generalizations from Galileo’s results. As a corollary Newton applied his laws of motion to demonstrate that forces must be added as vectors and showed how to do this geometrically with a parallelogram. The second law of motion states that the rate of change of momentum of a mass is proportional to the force acting upon it and takes place in the direction of the force. For the case of constant mass, this law serves as the definition of force $\mathbf{F}$ in terms of the acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ given to a mass $m$: $$ \begin{align*} {\color{green}\mathbf{F}} = m{\color{green}\mathbf{a}} \tag{1} \end{align*} $$

The unit of force in the SI system of units is the newton $(N)$. It is defined as the force that gives a mass of one kilogram $(1\ kg)$ an acceleration of $1\ ms^2$. His celebrated observation of a falling apple may be a legend, but Newton’s genius lay in recognizing that the type of gravitational field that caused the apple to fall was the same type that served to hold the Moon in its orbit around the Earth, the planets in their orbits around the Sun, and that acted between minute particles characterized only by their masses. If consider the Earth and falling apple as two particles, or point masses $m$ and $M$ separated by a distance $r$, we get for the gravitational attraction $F$ exerted by $M$ on $m$ $$ \begin{align*} {\color{green}\mathbf{F}} =G\times \frac{mM}{r^{2}} \times {\color{green}\mathbf{\hat{r}}} \tag{2} \end{align*} $$
In this equation $r̂$ is a unit vector in the direction of increase in coordinate $r$, which is directed away from the center of reference at the mass $M$. The negative sign in the equation indicates that the force $F$ acts in the opposite direction, toward the attracting mass $M$. The constant $G$, which converts the physical law to an equation, is the constant of universal gravitation. There was no way to determine the gravitational constant experimentally during Newton’s lifetime. The method to be followed was evident, namely to determine the force between two masses in a laboratory experiment. However, seventeenth century technology was not yet up to this task. Experimental determination of $G$ was extremely diﬃcult, and was first achieved more than a century after the publication of Principia by Lord Charles Cavendish (1731–1810). From a set of painstaking measurements of the force of attraction between two spheres of lead, Cavendish in 1798 determined the value of $G$ to be $6.754\times 10^{11} \ m^{3} \cdot kg^{-1} \cdot s^{-2}$. A modern value (Mohr and Taylor, 2005) is $6.674\ 210\ \times 10^{11} \ m^{3} \cdot kg^{-1} \cdot s^{-2}$. It has not yet been possible to determine G more precisely, due to experimental diﬃculty. Although other physical constants are now known with a relative standard uncer- tainty of much less than $1 \times 10^6$, the gravitational constant is known to only $150 \times 10^6$.

1.2) Gravitational Acceleration

In physics the field of a force is often more important than the absolute magnitude of the force. The field is defined as the force exerted on a material unit. For example, the electrical field of a charged body at a certain position is the force it exerts on a unit of electrical charge at that location. The gravitational field in the vicinity of an attracting mass is the force it exerts on a unit mass. Equation 1 shows that this is equivalent to the acceleration vector. In geophysical applications we are concerned with accelerations rather than forces. By comparing Eq. (1) and Eq. (2) we get the gravitational acceleration $g$ of the mass $m$ due to the attraction of the mass $M$: $$ \begin{align*} {\color{green}\mathbf{g}} =-G\times \frac{M}{r^{2}} \times {\color{green}\mathbf{\hat{r}}} \tag{3} \end{align*} $$

The SI unit of acceleration is the $m \cdot s^{-2}$; this unit is unpractical for use in geophysics. In the now superseded c.g.s. system the unit of acceleration was the $cm \cdot s^{-2}$, which is called a gal in recognition of the contributions of Galileo. The small changes in the acceleration of gravity caused by geological structures are measured in thousandths of this unit, i.e., in milligal (mgal). Until recently, gravity anomalies due to geological structures were surveyed with field instruments accurate to about one-tenth of a milligal, which was called a gravity unit. Modern instruments are capable of measuring gravity diﬀerences to a millionth of a gal, or microgal (mgal), which is becoming the practical unit of gravity investigations. The value of gravity at the Earth’s surface is about $9.8 \ m \cdot s^{-2}$, and so the sensitivity of modern measurements of gravity is about $1$ part in $109$.

1.2.1) Gravimeter: Absolute Gravity

In the design of gravimeters, practical measurements typically hinge on the acceleration of the free-fall object rather than directly on force. This practical approach employs Equation 3, enabling the computation of gravitational acceleration. Essentially, this translates into assessing variations in gravity from a predetermined reference level. For application in exploration geophysics, Eq. (3) needs to be simplified. The gravity term $g$ is substituted with $\delta g$ because of the tiny changes in gravity accerlation caused by the Earth's strata. Newton's original conception of planetary orbits in gravity attraction is significantly larger if compared with the internal Earth's crusts, so the gravity unit is implemented here. The negative sign in $-G$ is typically disregarded because most scenarios in exploration geophysics occur within the subsurface and the absolute direction of gravity points toward the Earth's cores. In a number of measurements, the unit vector $\mathbf{\hat{r}}$ can be assumed to be $1$ to avoid computation complexity. The target $M$ can be replaced with $\Delta M$, indicating that the density contrasts between the rock and its host is large enough to produce a distinguishable "anomaly." Thus, the Eq. (3) reformulates to Equation 4 and $\Delta M$ can relate to $\delta \rho \times v$. $$ \begin{align*} \delta g=G\times \frac{\Delta M}{r^{2}} =\frac{G}{r^{2}} \times \delta \rho \times v \tag{4} \end{align*} $$

$$ \begin{align*} \delta g\ & =gravity\ attraction\ between\ weight\ drop\ and\ rocks\\ G\ & =universal\ gravitational\ constant\ \left( 6.674\times 10^{-11} ,\ m^{3} \cdot kg^{-1} \cdot s^{-2}\right)\\ \Delta M\ & =mass\ contrast\ between\ rock\ and\ its\ host\\ r\ & =distance\ between\ the\ rock\ and\ weight\ drop\\ \delta \rho \ & =density\ contrast\\ v\ & =volume\ of\ the\ rock \end{align*} $$

1.3.3) Gravitational Vector: Point Mass
Gravity, being a force, inherently has both magnitude and direction. Considering it as a vector rather than an absolute value (scalar) is crucial for accurately describing its effects on objects. The direction of the gravitational force is essential for calculating the gravitational tensor and can provide inside information on gravitational anisotropy. In addition, it is crucial in situations where multiple forces are acting on an object, as the net force is the vector sum of all individual forces.
1.3.4) Spherical Model for Depth and Target Size Analysis
The theoretical computation of gravitational attraction induced by a subsurface anomaly can be ascertained using Equation 3. Generally, the spatial variations of gravity in the x and y axes are negligible, and most gravimeters are not designed to detect them. The most substantial gravitational force occurs along the z-axis, directed towards the Earth's core. This is due to the mass distribution within the Earth, with density variations and anomalies contributing to the changes in the gravitational field. 1. $m_{sphere}$: This is the mass of the sphere, which is calculated by multiplying the volume of the sphere $v$ by the change in density $\Delta \rho_{contrast}$ between the sphere and its surrounding medium. The volume of a sphere is given by $\frac{4}{3} \pi R^3$, where $R$ is the radius of the sphere. 2. $cos(\theta)$: This is the cosine of the angle $\theta$ between the radial line $r$ from the center of the sphere to the observation point and the vertical $z$ axis. 3. $\Delta g_{z}$: This is the change in the gravitational field in the z-direction at the observation point due to the presence of the sphere. 4. $\Delta g_{net}$: This is the net change in the gravitational field at the observation point due to the presence of the sphere. 5. $G$: This is the gravitational constant. 6. $r$: This is the radial distance from the center of the sphere to the observation point. It is calculated as $\sqrt{x^2 + z^2}$, where $x$ is the horizontal distance from the center of the sphere to the observation point and $z$ is the vertical distance from the center of the sphere to the observation point. 7. $\Delta g_{z}$: The final expression for $\Delta g_{z}$ is the change in the gravitational field in the z-direction at the observation point due to the presence of the sphere. It is calculated by multiplying the net change in the gravitational field $\Delta g_{net}$ by the cosine of the angle between the radial line and the vertical axis $\cos(\theta) = \frac{z}{r}$, and substituting $\Delta g_{net}$ by $\frac{G m_{sphere}}{r^2}$. The final equation for $\Delta g_{z}$ expresses the change in the gravitational field in the z-direction at the observation point due to a sphere of radius \(R\), density contrast $\Delta \rho_{contrast}$, and located at a distance $\sqrt{x^2 + z^2}$ from the observation point. Using the code snippet below to compute the gravitational attraction in one point observation in z-axis. ```python import numpy as np G = 6.674*pow(10, -11) gold_density = 19300 # kg/m3 sandstone_density = 2590 # kg/m3 density_contrast = gold_density - sandstone_density gold_diameter = 100 # meters gold_depth = 200 # meters distance_x = 500 # meters def gravity_sphere(G, density_contrast, R, z, x): x1 = density_contrast * ((4/3) * np.pi * pow(R, 3)) * z x2 = pow((pow(x, 2) + pow(z, 2)), 1.5) gz = G * (x1/x2) gal = gz*0.01 # unit gal mgal = gal*pow(10, 6) # unit gal return mgal mgal_gz = gravity_sphere(G, density_contrast, gold_diameter/2, gold_depth, distance_x) print(f"gravity at {distance_x} meters from the origin is: {mgal_gz} mgal") ```
1.3.5) Gravitational Acceleration and Potential
The law of superposition is a fundamental principle in various branches of physics, and gravitation is no exception. In the context of gravitation, the principle asserts that the gravitational force or acceleration due to multiple point masses is essentially the vector sum of forces or accelerations produced by each point mass. While this approach requires vectorial manipulations, gravitation can be alternatively conceptualized as a scalar field, a perspective that offers a more straightforward treatment of diverse mass distributions. By incorporating the concept of gravitational potential, we can refine our understanding. The gravitational potential at a point in space, due to a point mass, represents the work done in bringing a unit mass from infinity to that point against gravitational forces. When dealing with multiple point masses, the total gravitational potential at a given location is simply the algebraic sum (or scalar sum) of the potentials due to each of these masses. This scalar approach aligns with the law of superposition, suggesting that the effects of individual masses are additive. This scalar perspective, encapsulated by gravitational potential, is particularly convenient. It bypasses the intricacies of vector operations and provides a direct, additive method to account for gravitational influences of complex and arbitrary mass distributions. By focusing on gravitational potential, we can grasp the cumulative effect of numerous point masses or continuous mass distributions more effortlessly than individually vector-summing the forces or accelerations they induce. $$ V_{g} =G\int _{mass}\frac{\partial m}{r} \approx G\sum _{j}\frac{m_{j}}{r_{j}} \tag{6} $$ $$ \begin{align*} V_{g} & :\ gravitational\ potential\\ G & :\ universal\ gravitational\ constant\\ m & :\ point\ mass\\ r & :\ distance\ between\ observation\ and\ point\ mass\\ & :\ \sqrt{( x_{2} -x_{1})^{2} +( y_{2} -y_{1})^{2}} \end{align*} $$
The utilization of gravity potential is a valuable approach when encountering multiple gravity anomalies. This technique analyzes scattering point masses that are uniformly distributed across a subsurface target. However, in most geophysics exploration, mineral deposits comprise a wide range of mineral forms, which can introduce heterogeneity in the point mass principle. As a result, it is crucial to consider this variability when applying the gravity potential concept in geophysics exploration. ```python import numpy as np import math import matplotlib.pyplot as plt G = 6.674*pow(10, -11) density = 19300 # kg/m3 area = 77*71 gold_mass = area*density station_1 = np.array([580, 0]) station_2 = np.array([856, 0]) station_3 = np.array([1150, 0]) station_4 = np.array([1434, 0]) station_5 = np.array([1718, 0]) point_masses = np.array([[1005, 737], [1031, 784], [1109, 771], [997, 830], [1065, 835], [1169, 819], [1129, 874], [1040, 896]]) def distance(point1, point2): x1, y1 = point1 x2, y2 = point2 return math.sqrt((x2 - x1)**2 + (y2 - y1)**2) def potential(G, gold_mass, dis): return G*(gold_mass/dis) total_U = 0 # initialize the accumulator for point_mass in point_masses: dis = distance(station_1, point_mass) U = potential(G, gold_mass, dis) print(f"point mass at: {point_mass} distance: {dis} meter give potential: {U} j/kg") total_U += U # accumulate the potential print(total_U) ```
1.3.6) Understanding the Geoid: Gravitational Anomalies and Earth's Irregular Shape
The concept of a gravity anomaly and the geoid are both essential in understanding the Earth's gravitational field and its underlying structures. Here's a refined explanation: A gravity anomaly is the deviation between the observed gravitational measurement at a particular location on Earth's surface and the value predicted by a theoretical model. This discrepancy arises due to the Earth's non-uniform density and topographical variations. If the Earth were a perfect oblate spheroid with uniform density, gravitational measurements would adhere to a simple algebraic expression. But with factors like altitude, nearby terrains, and the presence of dense subsurface structures, gravitational anomalies occur. These anomalies are especially revealing; for instance, a subterranean ore deposit can induce a positive anomaly because of its heightened gravitational pull. There are various theoretical models used to predict gravity values, and the gravity anomaly is often contextualized concerning a chosen model. Examples include the Bouguer, free-air, and isostatic gravity anomalies, each factoring in distinct theoretical adjustments to gravitational values. By conducting gravity surveys with gravimeters, geologists can determine these anomalies over an area and deduce the region's subsurface geology. While gravity anomalies delve into deviations in gravitational measurements, the geoid presents an overarching understanding of the Earth's gravitational field. The geoid represents the hypothetical shape the Earth's sea surface would assume under the sole influence of Earth's gravitational pull and rotation, excluding other factors like winds and tides. This "mathematical figure of the Earth" is an irregular, smoothed-out surface, revealing the discrepancies arising from the uneven distribution of mass both within and on Earth's surface. Although it's a conceptual surface, the geoid is a vital reference in geodesy and geophysics, offering insights into Earth's true gravitational shape and variations, determined through extensive gravitational observations and computations.
