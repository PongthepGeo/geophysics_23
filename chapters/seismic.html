1) Introduction

seismic survey, method of investigating subterranean structure, particularly as related to exploration for petroleum, natural gas, and mineral deposits. The technique is based on determining the time interval that elapses between the initiation of a seismic wave at a selected shot point (the location where an explosion generates seismic waves) and the arrival of reflected or refracted impulses at one or more seismic detectors. Seismic air guns are commonly used to initiate the seismic waves. This technique has largely replaced the practice of exploding dynamite underground. Electric vibrators or falling weights (thumpers) may also be employed at sites where an underground explosion might cause damage—e.g., where caverns are present. Upon arrival at the detectors, the amplitude and timing of waves are recorded to give a seismogram (record of ground vibrations). Generally, the density of rocks near the surface of Earth increases with depth. Seismic waves initiated at a shot point at or near the surface may reach the receiving point by reflection, refraction, or both. When the shot point is close to the receiving point, reflected waves usually reach the receiving point first. At greater distances, however, the seismic pulse travels faster by the refraction path because its velocity is greater along the top of the lower, denser layer than it is through the upper layer; in this case, the refracted wave arrives first. Interpretation of the depths and media reached by seismic waves thus depends on the distance between shot points and receiving points and the densities of the strata. The results of a seismic survey may be presented in the form of a cross-sectional drawing of the subsurface structures as if cut by a plane through the shot point, the detector, and Earth’s centre. Such drawings are called seismic profiles.

1.1) Understanding Seismic Image

When comparing seismic images with photographic images, it's essential to note the significant disparities in resolution. The resolution effectively describes an image's detail and is often considerably higher in photographic images than seismic images. Photographic imagery is dependent on the interaction of light waves with objects. This interaction occurs when light waves from a source, such as the Sun, propagate to the objects and reflect back to the camera sensor, where they are recorded. Because the waves travel through the air, they experience minimal attenuation and maintain their high frequencies, resulting in high-resolution images. Seismic images inherently possess lower resolution due to the nature of the seismic waves and the medium they travel through, compared to photographic images. The seismic waves used for imaging typically fall within a lower frequency range, such as 5-80 Hz for vibroseis and 10-300 Hz for airguns used in marine exploration. These mechanical waves propagate through the Earth's strata, which are characterized by high-attenuation properties that result from the complex and heterogeneous nature of geological formations. This medium, consisting of rocks and various substrates, tends to absorb and scatter the seismic energy, thereby reducing the quality and resolution of the returned signal that is eventually used for imaging the subsurface structures. On the other hand, photographic images are formed by electromagnetic waves traveling through a low attenuation medium, such as air, resulting in higher resolution.

1.2.3) Fourier Series
The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is a mathematical technique used in signal processing and many other fields to analyze the frequency content of a discrete signal. It's fundamentally a transformation used to convert a signal from its time or spatial domain into its frequency domain. The DFT converts a sequence of complex numbers $x_n$ into another sequence of complex numbers $x_k$. In the context of the equation you've provided: $$$ f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_{n} \cos\left(\frac{2\pi nt}{P}\right) + b_{n} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi nt}{P}\right)\right] \tag{2} $$$ This equation represents a Fourier series, which is a way to represent a periodic function as a sum of sine and cosine functions. The Fourier series is particularly useful for analyzing functions that are periodic over a certain interval. Contrasting with DFT: The DFT is defined by the formula: $$$ X_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n \cdot e^{-i2\pi kn/N} \tag{3} $$$ where: - $X_k$ are the DFT coefficients (output of the DFT), - $x_n$ are the input sequence values, - $N$ is the total number of samples, - $k$ is the index of the DFT output, and - $i$ is the imaginary unit. This transformation is used when the function \(f(t)\) is sampled at discrete points in time (or space) and is assumed to be periodic for the purpose of the analysis. Key Points about DFT: 1. **Discrete and Finite**: DFT deals with discrete signals which are finite. 2. **Periodicity**: Assumes that the input signal is periodic and continues indefinitely. 3. **Complex Exponential**: Uses complex exponentials as the basis functions. 4. **Complex Output**: The DFT produces a sequence of complex numbers, which represent the amplitude and phase of sinusoids at different frequencies. 5. **Computational Efficiency**: Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm is often used to compute the DFT efficiently. 6. **Frequency Resolution**: The frequency resolution of the DFT is determined by the length of the input signal. More points yield better frequency resolution. 7. **Amplitude and Phase Information**: Provides both amplitude and phase information for each frequency component in the signal. Applications: - **Signal Analysis**: To analyze the frequency content of signals. - **Data Compression**: For example, in image compression, like in the JPEG format. - **Signal Filtering**: To design filters in the frequency domain, which are then applied to signals in the time domain. - **Spectral Analysis**: To examine the power spectrum of signals. - **Telecommunications**: For modulation and demodulation in communication systems. - **Image Processing**: For filtering and reconstructing images. The DFT is fundamental to many digital signal processing applications. It enables the representation of a signal in terms of its frequency content, facilitating numerous operations like filtering, compression, and analysis.

2.1) Seismic Wave Characteristics

2.1.1) P-waves
In the Figure 6 illustrating P-wave propagation, the transmitted energy moves through the medium, causing particles to undergo compressional and dilational motions. The energy direction is parallel to the particle motion axis. As the wave passes, particles are first compressed (squeezed), and then released, leading to dilation. It's important to note that while the rock medium may momentarily alter its features during this process, it eventually returns to its original shape due to its elastic nature.
2.1.2) S-waves
S-waves propagate linearly along a distinct axis, but the induced particle displacements occur perpendicular to this propagation direction. These orthogonal oscillations manifest as vertical (SV) and horizontal (SH) motions relative to the wave's trajectory. Notably, Eath's materials such as gases and liquids lack the rigidity to support these shear motions, thereby inhibiting the transmission of S-waves through them.

3.1) Wave Phenomena: Reflection

The provided illustration depicts the symmetrical propagation of P-waves (Primary or Pressure waves) as they spread through a homogeneous medium, originating from a central point. If these waves encounter a boundary, they can reflect back towards the surface. This phenomenon is appropriately termed "reflection." The uniformity of the medium ensures that the waves move consistently in all directions, and any deviations from this symmetrical pattern can indicate variations or anomalies within the medium.

3.2) Wave Phenomena: Interbed Multiple

Multiple generally refers to the wave phenomenon that reflects more than one time. Our scenario shows that the reflected waves at time step 200 ms reflect at the first layer and propagate downward to the second layer. The wave can oscillate in the sandstone layer more than one time. Conventional seismic processing considers multiple as noise; duplicating the first layer (in this scenario) caused an artifact of the fake layer. In wave physics, the term "multiples" describes a phenomenon where seismic waves undergo several reflections before being recorded. This process can occur when a wave reflects off an interface more than once, creating an oscillatory path within the layers of the subsurface. In the scenario presented, the snapshots taken at time step 200 ms reveal the occurrence of multiples. The seismic waves, initially reflected from the first lithological layer, travel downward and subsequently interact with the interface between the first and second layers. Following this interaction, the waves are reflected into the sandstone layer, potentially undergoing additional reflections. As a result, these waves can oscillate multiple times within the sandstone stratum before ultimately being recorded or dissipating. Conventional seismic processing often categorizes multiples as noise, given that they can obscure or distort the true subsurface image. In this context, the multiple reflections within the sandstone layer can create an artifact that mimics the presence of an additional, non-existent layer. This pseudo-layer is a byproduct of the repeated reflections and can lead to misinterpretations in seismic data analysis. Hence, understanding and accurately identifying multiples is crucial in seismic processing to ensure the fidelity of subsurface imaging and to prevent the introduction of artifacts that can compromise the integrity of geological interpretations. The Huygens–Fresnel principle (named after Dutch physicist Christiaan Huygens and French physicist Augustin-Jean Fresnel) states that every point on a wavefront is itself the source of spherical wavelets, and the secondary wavelets emanating from different points mutually interfere. The sum of these spherical wavelets forms a new wavefront. As such, the Huygens-Fresnel principle is a method of analysis applied to problems of luminous wave propagation both in the far-field limit and in near-field diffraction as well as reflection.
